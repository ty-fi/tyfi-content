# Cover Letter Template (General)

## Structure (3–4 paragraphs, max 1 page)

### Header
```
[Your Name]
[City, State ZIP] | [Phone] | [Email] | [LinkedIn/Portfolio URL]

[Date]

[Hiring Manager Name or "Hiring Manager"]
[Organization Name]
[Address, if available]

Dear [Name or "Hiring Manager"],
```

---

### Paragraph 1: The Hook (2–3 sentences)
**Purpose:** Grab attention immediately. Lead with your strongest qualification or a compelling connection to the org's mission.

**Formula:** [One-sentence achievement that relates to the role] + [Why this specific organization excites you] + [Brief statement of what you bring].

**Example formula:**
> When [specific event/publication/recent news about org], I [your connection/insight]. As [your identity relative to the role], I [one thing that makes you uniquely qualified].

**Avoid:** Generic openings ("I am writing to apply...") or clichés ("I am a detail-oriented self-starter...").

---

### Paragraph 2: The Evidence (4–6 sentences)
**Purpose:** Prove you can do the job. Connect your experience directly to the job's requirements using specific examples.

**Structure:**
1. Identify a key job requirement
2. Describe a relevant accomplishment (with measurable impact if possible)
3. Connect it back to what the organization needs

**Formula:** In my role at [Organization], I [specific action] that resulted in [measurable outcome]. This experience taught me [insight] — directly relevant to [job requirement at target org].

**Tips:**
- Use 2–3 examples if space permits
- Match terminology from the job posting
- Show impact, not just tasks
- If you have gaps, frame adjacent experience as transferable

---

### Paragraph 3: The Fit (3–4 sentences)
**Purpose:** Demonstrate you understand the organization's work and explain why you're the right person at this moment.

**Elements to include:**
- Reference to specific work the org has done (shows research)
- Alignment with values/mission
- What you'll bring that complements the team

**Formula:** [Org's work you admire] aligns with my [background/value/commitment]. I am particularly drawn to [specific aspect of role/org]. My experience [x] would complement your team's [y].

---

### Paragraph 4: The Close (2–3 sentences)
**Purpose:** Reiterate enthusiasm and prompt next steps.

**Formula:** I would welcome the opportunity to discuss how my experience [specific strength] could support [Org's mission/project]. Thank you for your consideration.

**Sign-off:**
> Best regards,
> [Your Name]

---

## Key Principles from Research

1. **Tailor every letter** — Generic letters are obvious and ineffective. Use the job posting's language.
2. **Lead with achievement, not credentials** — "I led an analysis that saved ratepayers $X" beats "I have 7 years of experience."
3. **Tell stories, don't list skills** — A specific example demonstrates competence; "I'm a strategic thinker" is empty.
4. **Match the voice to the org** — A progressive think tank warrant a more mission-driven tone than a corporate utility.
5. **Address someone by name if possible** — Shows initiative. If you can't find a name, use "Dear Hiring Manager" (not "To Whom It May Concern").
6. **Use keywords from the posting** — Helps with ATS and shows you read carefully.
7. **Keep it to one page** — 3–4 paragraphs, concise.
8. **End with a call to action** — Express enthusiasm and openness to conversation.

---

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- Repeating your resume verbatim
- Using cliché phrases ("think outside the box," "team player," "passionate about")
- Focusing on what you want (learning, growth) instead of what you offer
- Being too formal or too casual for the org's culture
- Including irrelevant information
- Typos or formatting inconsistencies
- Not researching the org deeply enough
